\section{Predictive Value of Sentiment}
\label{sec:predictive}

The preceding sections established that the sentiment series is coherent, structurally differentiated, and leads the policy rate around decision events. We now ask whether the textual signal predicts the \emph{direction} of the next \gls{ecb} rate decision once the rate history is controlled for. Section~\ref{sec:results_correlations} characterises the lag-and-era correlation structure that motivates feature selection. Section~\ref{sec:results_prediction} fits and compares four classifiers on the meeting-level rate-decision direction. Section~\ref{sec:results_surprise} closes the chapter with an analysis of whether communication surprises move markets contemporaneously.

\subsection{Cross-Era Predictive Correlations}
\label{sec:results_correlations}

Figure~\ref{fig:lag_profile} traces the Pearson correlation between three selected sentiment features and the contemporaneous \gls{mro} change across horizons of zero to fifteen meetings (changed meetings only, $n \approx 54$). Two patterns emerge. First, the correlation is already small but significant at lag~$0$ ($r = 0.31$, $p < 0.05$ for the FinBERT overall mean); it rises to a pronounced peak at lag~$4$ ($r = 0.551$). Second, the profile is non-monotonic, with a secondary plateau through lags~$6$--$10$, indicating that sentiment carries predictive content at multiple horizons. The three features peak at different lags---FinBERT IS at lag~$2$ ($r = 0.352$), and CentralBankRoBERTa IS at lag~$9$ ($r = 0.409$)---so the textual signal is not a single-horizon lead.

\subsection{Predicting the Direction of ECB Rate Decisions}
\label{sec:results_prediction}

The predictive task is a three-class classification problem. Each press conference is assigned $y_t \in \{-1, 0, +1\}$ corresponding to the sign of the \gls{mro} change: $-1$ if cut, $0$ if held, and $+1$ if hiked. The class distribution is heavily skewed towards \emph{hold} ($79.3\%$ of $279$ meetings), setting a demanding naive baseline.

Four classifiers are compared. \textbf{M1 (Baseline)} uses rate history only: $\{\Delta\text{MRO}_{t-1},\,\allowbreak \text{MRO}_{t-1}\}$. \textbf{M2 (Sentiment)} uses four features---FinBERT overall mean at lags $2$ and $4$, FinBERT IS maximum at lag $2$, and FinBERT IS standard deviation---isolating the marginal content of the textual signal. \textbf{M3 (Augmented)} combines M1 and M2 features. \textbf{M4 (Random Forest)} applies a $400$-tree ensemble with restricted depth ($\texttt{max\_depth}=4$) to the M3 feature set, relaxing the linearity assumption. All models use class-balanced loss weights and $5$-fold time-series cross-validation via \textit{scikit-learn}'s \textit{TimeSeriesSplit}, which constructs five strictly forward-looking, expanding-window train/test splits. The test fold of every split is chronologically posterior to its training fold, so no future information enters the feature set at training time.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{images/results/model/fig_performance.pdf}
    \caption[Cross-validated performance of the four predictive models]{Cross-validated performance ($n = 277$--$279$).}
    \label{fig:performance}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:performance} reports the results. Three observations follow. First, M2's raw accuracy ($0.39 \pm 0.11$) falls well below the naive baseline---the expected outcome of a class-balanced classifier on an imbalanced sample. With Hold meetings comprising $79.3\%$ of the sample, the always-Hold predictor achieves an accuracy of $0.793$ that no class-balanced classifier can mechanically match; we therefore report AUC (one-vs-rest, macro-averaged) as the primary metric, since it is invariant to the class prior and rewards correct directional ranking rather than majority-class prediction. Second, the AUC ordering is strict: M1 ($0.682$) $<$ M2 ($0.694$) $<$ M3 ($0.705$) $<$ M4 ($0.763$). Third, and most strikingly, the largest single increment is M3$\rightarrow$M4 ($+0.058$): the Random Forest captures non-linear interactions that the logistic specification cannot, an interpretation confirmed by the importance profile in Figure~\ref{fig:rf_importances}.

Figure~\ref{fig:comparison_m3_m4} places the M3 and M4 confusion matrices side by side; Table~\ref{tab:classification_v2} reports the underlying precision, recall, and F1 scores.

\subsection{Communication Surprise and Market Volatility}
\label{sec:results_surprise}

The preceding analysis asked whether lagged sentiment predicts future rate
decisions. A complementary question is whether the \emph{change} in sentiment
from one meeting to the next---the communication surprise---correlates with the
contemporaneous market reaction, and whether the \emph{dispersion} of sentiment
within a press conference predicts the size of that reaction. Two estimation
strategies address these questions: a static full-sample Pearson correlation
and a 20-meeting rolling window that reveals regime-level variation.
Figure~\ref{fig:delta_sentiment} reports the static checks.

        Points colour-coded by era: blue = Pre-ZLB (1999--2011),

    \label{fig:delta_sentiment}

\subsubsection*{Static correlations}

Three results emerge from the full-sample analysis. First, all directional
correlations (top row) are statistically indistinguishable from zero: the
first-differenced \gls{is} mean ($r = +0.037$, n.s.), \gls{qa} mean
($r = +0.035$, n.s.), and overall FinBERT score ($r = +0.052$, n.s.) carry no
systematic information about the sign of the same-day \gls{ois}-1Y move. A
hawkish communication surprise did not reliably produce a positive rate
reaction at the one-year horizon.

Second, the within-conference sentiment dispersion features---proxies for
the internal coherence of the press conference---are likewise uninformative
about market volatility in the aggregate: FinBERT \gls{is} standard deviation
($r = -0.020$, n.s.) and CentralBankRoBERTa \gls{is} standard deviation
($r = -0.015$, n.s.) produce near-zero correlations with
$|\Delta\overline{\text{OIS}}|$. Third, sentiment \emph{levels}, rather
than dispersions, show a weak but statistically significant negative
association with market volatility: FinBERT \gls{is} mean
($r = -0.146$, $p < 0.05$), FinBERT overall mean ($r = -0.125$, $p < 0.05$),
and CentralBankRoBERTa overall standard deviation
($r = -0.130$, $p < 0.05$). The sign is consistent across models: more
positive or more coherent ECB communication is associated with \emph{smaller}
absolute market reactions, suggesting that communication clarity reduces
pricing uncertainty rather than amplifying it. This pattern echoes the
communicative-cancellation finding of Section~\ref{sec:divergence}.

\subsubsection*{Time-varying structure}

The static aggregate conceals a strongly regime-dependent structure.
Figure~\ref{fig:rolling_correlation} traces the same directional and
volatility correlations through a 20-meeting rolling window
(${\approx}\,2.5$ years at eight meetings per year). The dotted horizontal
lines mark the two-sided 95\,\% significance threshold for $n = 20$
($r_{\text{crit}} = \pm 0.444$); segments outside the corridor are
statistically distinguishable from zero at the local-window level, while
segments inside should be read as noise.

The top panel reveals three structurally distinct regimes. In the pre-\gls{gfc}
period the \gls{is} surprise correlation peaks at approximately $+0.50$,
marginally clearing the significance corridor: a hawkish tone shift moved
one-year rates upward on impact, consistent with an environment in which
markets treated ECB communication as a reliable signal of the near-term policy
path. This relationship collapsed during the \gls{gfc} and turned persistently
and significantly negative through the \gls{zlb} era, reaching a trough of
approximately $-0.50$ to $-0.60$ during 2010--2013: under forward guidance and
the effective lower bound, a hawkish communication surprise was re-interpreted
as a reassurance about the economic outlook rather than a tightening signal,
producing the opposite market response. The hiking cycle (2022--2024) shows
a tentative recovery toward positive values, though the rolling correlation
remains inside the significance corridor for most of that period, indicating
that the sign reversal had not yet fully stabilised by the end of the sample.
