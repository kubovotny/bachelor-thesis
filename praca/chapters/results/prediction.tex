\section{Predictive Value of Sentiment}
\label{sec:predictive}

The preceding sections established that the sentiment series is coherent, structurally differentiated, and leads the policy rate around decision events. We now ask whether the textual signal predicts the \emph{direction} of the next \gls{ecb} rate decision once the rate history is controlled for. Section~\ref{sec:results_correlations} characterises the lag-and-era correlation structure that motivates feature selection. Section~\ref{sec:results_prediction} fits and compares four classifiers on the meeting-level rate-decision direction. Section~\ref{sec:results_surprise} closes the chapter with an analysis of whether communication surprises move markets contemporaneously.

\subsection{Cross-Era Predictive Correlations}
\label{sec:results_correlations}

Figure~\ref{fig:lag_profile} traces the Pearson correlation between three selected sentiment features and the contemporaneous \gls{mro} change across horizons of zero to fifteen meetings (changed meetings only, $n \approx 54$). Two patterns emerge. First, the correlation is already small but significant at lag~$0$ ($r = 0.31$, $p < 0.05$ for the FinBERT overall mean); it rises to a pronounced peak at lag~$4$ ($r = 0.551$). Second, the profile is non-monotonic, with a secondary plateau through lags~$6$--$10$, indicating that sentiment carries predictive content at multiple horizons. The three features peak at different lags---FinBERT IS at lag~$2$ ($r = 0.352$), and CentralBankRoBERTa IS at lag~$9$ ($r = 0.409$)---so the textual signal is not a single-horizon lead.

Table~\ref{tab:summary} disaggregates the signal across monetary regimes. Three regularities emerge. \subsection{Predicting the Direction of ECB Rate Decisions}
\label{sec:results_prediction}

The predictive task is a three-class classification problem. Each press conference is assigned $y_t \in \{-1, 0, +1\}$ corresponding to the sign of the \gls{mro} change: $-1$ if cut, $0$ if held, and $+1$ if hiked. The class distribution is heavily skewed towards \emph{hold} ($79.3\%$ of $279$ meetings), setting a demanding naive baseline.

Four classifiers are compared. \textbf{M1 (Baseline)} uses rate history only: $\{\Delta\text{MRO}_{t-1},\,\allowbreak \text{MRO}_{t-1}\}$. \textbf{M2 (Sentiment)} uses four features---FinBERT overall mean at lags $2$ and $4$, FinBERT IS maximum at lag $2$, and FinBERT IS standard deviation---isolating the marginal content of the textual signal. \textbf{M3 (Augmented)} combines M1 and M2 features. \textbf{M4 (Random Forest)} applies a $400$-tree ensemble with restricted depth ($\texttt{max\_depth}=4$) to the M3 feature set, relaxing the linearity assumption. All models use class-balanced loss weights and $5$-fold time-series cross-validation.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{images/results/model/fig_performance.pdf}
    \caption[Cross-validated performance of the four predictive models]{Cross-validated performance ($n = 277$--$279$).}
    \label{fig:performance}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:performance} reports the results. Three observations follow. First, M2's raw accuracy ($0.39 \pm 0.11$) falls well below the naive baseline---the expected outcome of a class-balanced classifier on an imbalanced sample; AUC is therefore the primary metric. Second, the AUC ordering is strict: M1 ($0.682$) $<$ M2 ($0.694$) $<$ M3 ($0.705$) $<$ M4 ($0.763$). Third, and most strikingly, the largest single increment is M3$\rightarrow$M4 ($+0.058$): the Random Forest captures non-linear interactions that the logistic specification cannot, an interpretation confirmed by the importance profile in Figure~\ref{fig:rf_importances}.

Figure~\ref{fig:comparison_m3_m4} places the M3 and M4 confusion matrices side by side; Table~\ref{tab:classification_v2} reports the underlying precision, recall, and F1 scores.

The coefficient and importance profiles are mutually consistent. In M3, $\Delta\text{MRO}_{t-1}$ is the sharpest short-horizon predictor: strongly positive on \textit{hike}, strongly negative on \textit{cut}. $\text{MRO}_{t-1}$ loads positive on \textit{hold}, capturing the \gls{ecb}'s low sequential reversal rate. Among sentiment features, FinBERT IS maximum at lag~$2$ carries the largest hike coefficient (standardised $\approx +1.0$): the most extreme hawkish passage in the IS two meetings prior is the strongest textual precursor of tightening. \subsection{Communication Surprise and Market Volatility}
\label{sec:results_surprise}

The preceding analysis asked whether lagged sentiment predicts future rate decisions. A complementary question is whether the \emph{change} in sentiment from one meeting to the next---the communication surprise---correlates with the contemporaneous market reaction. Figure~\ref{fig:delta_sentiment} reports two static checks across the full sample.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/model/fig_delta_sentiment.pdf}
    \caption[Communication surprise and market volatility]{Top: $\Delta$sentiment vs.\ \gls{ois}-1Y change ($n = 271$). Bottom: sentiment std vs.\ $|\Delta\text{OIS}|$ ($n = 272$). Points colour-coded by era.}
    \label{fig:delta_sentiment}
\end{figure}

Two results emerge. First, all three directional correlations (top row) are statistically indistinguishable from zero ($r = 0.037$, $0.035$, $0.052$, all n.s.): a hawkish surprise did not systematically produce a positive \gls{ois} move on the same day. Second, the CentralBankRoBERTa overall standard deviation is weakly negatively correlated with the absolute \gls{ois} move ($r = -0.130$, $p < 0.05$, bottom-right panel): internally variable communication is associated with smaller market reactions, echoing the communicative-cancellation finding of Section~\ref{sec:divergence}.

The static aggregate, however, conceals a strongly regime-dependent structure. Figure~\ref{fig:rolling_correlation} traces the same correlations through a $20$-meeting rolling window ($\approx 2.5$ years).

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{%
        images/results/model/fig_rolling_correlation.pdf}
    \caption[Time-varying sentiment--market correlation]{Rolling Pearson correlation (20-meeting window). Top: $\Delta$sentiment vs.\ \gls{ois}-1Y. Bottom: sentiment std vs.\ $|\Delta\text{OIS}|$.}
    \label{fig:rolling_correlation}
\end{figure}

In the pre-\gls{gfc} period the IS surprise correlation is strongly positive (${\approx}+0.50$): a hawkish tone shift moved rates upward on the same day. This relationship collapsed during the \gls{gfc} and turned persistently negative through the \gls{zlb} era (${\approx}{-0.50}$ by 2010--2012): under forward guidance, a hawkish surprise was interpreted as reassurance about the economic outlook, not as a tightening signal, producing the opposite market reaction. The hiking cycle shows a partial recovery toward positive values by 2024. The near-zero full-sample correlation in Figure~\ref{fig:delta_sentiment} is therefore an artefact of aggregation: pre-\gls{gfc} positive effects and
\gls{zlb}-era negative effects cancel out across the full sample.

The volatility relationship (bottom panel) is similarly unstable: near zero in normal times, spiking to $+0.50$--$0.60$ during the \gls{gfc} and mid-\gls{zlb} crisis periods, and collapsing or reversing during the hiking cycle when the \gls{ecb} communicated with unusual clarity while producing the largest \gls{ois} moves in the sample. The regime instability is itself an economically meaningful finding: the informational value of communication surprises depends critically on the institutional context.
