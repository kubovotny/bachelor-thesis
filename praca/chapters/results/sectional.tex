\section{The Sectional Anatomy of Policy Communication}
\label{sec:results_sectional}

The headline series collapses the rich internal structure of a press conference into a single number. We now exploit the sectional split documented in Section~\ref{sec:data_qa_construction} to ask whether the prepared and spontaneous components communicate different information and whether any structural gap between them carries market-moving content.

%─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{IS versus Q\&A: Two Different Documents}
\label{sec:results_is_vs_qa}

Figure~\ref{fig:IS_vs_QA} plots the two sections separately across the full sample. Three regularities are immediately visible. First, IS sentiment dominates QA sentiment at almost every date for both models. Second, the IS series is markedly more volatile---the IQR ratio documented in Table~\ref{tab:descriptives} is approximately three to one. Third, the two series move broadly in the same direction at low frequency but diverge at higher frequencies, a pattern already quantified in Table~\ref{tab:is_qa_corr}: both classifiers explain less than $7\%$ of the other section's variance (FinBERT: $R^{2} = 0.059$; CB-RoBERTa: $R^{2} = 0.066$). Approximately $93-94\, \%$ of the \gls{qa}'s variance is orthogonal to the \gls{is}.

    \caption[Prepared versus spontaneous communication]{Introductory Statement versus Q\&A sentiment over time. Left: FinBERT; right: CentralBankRoBERTa.}

\end{figure}

\subsection{Internal Structure of the Press Conference}
\label{sec:results_arc}

Because the data are segmented into short chunks, we can track how sentiment evolves
\emph{within} each section. Figure~\ref{fig:intra_section} bins the chunks by their relative position and averages across all meetings ($n_{\textsc{is}} = 289$; $n_{\textsc{qa}} = 281$).

\begin{figure}[htp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/is_qa/fig_intra_section_both.pdf}
    \caption[Intra-section sentiment trajectory]{Intra-section sentiment dynamics. Shaded: $\pm 1$ SE.}
    \label{fig:intra_section}
\end{figure}

The IS profile traces a coherent three-act structure: it opens at a moderate level, dips between the one-fifth and two-fifths marks where the \gls{ecb} catalogues downside risks, then rises sharply through the final passages where the policy decision and forward guidance are stated---a swing of approximately $0.4$ sentiment units in FinBERT from trough to close. The Q\&A profile, by contrast, is essentially flat: a sharp question lands as easily in the first exchange as in the last, and treating the Q\&A as a single narrative document averages over a random sequence of topics rather than a coherent arc. This is the empirical justification for the chunk-level aggregation operator of Section~\ref{sec:aggregation}.

The right panel also reveals a stable two-tier structure within the Q\&A. \gls{ecb} answers score consistently above journalist questions for both models, with mean gaps of $0.14$ (FinBERT) and $0.29$ (CentralBankRoBERTa). Figure~\ref{fig:QA_distribution} reports the CB-RoBERTa gap, on which the asymmetry is more pronounced; the FinBERT counterpart shows the same sign but a smaller magnitude.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/is_qa/fig_QA_distribution.pdf}
    \caption[Distribution of the Q--A sentiment gap]{Journalist-minus-\gls{ecb} sentiment gap (CentralBankRoBERTa). Left: full-sample histogram. Right: absolute gap by monetary regime.}
    \label{fig:QA_distribution}
\end{figure}

The signed gap $S_Q - S_A$ has a mean of $-0.286$ and a median of $-0.238$; journalists are more dovish than the \gls{ecb} in $77\%$ of question--answer pairs. The right panel shows that the absolute gap is stable across eras, with mean values near $0.30$ in both the Pre-\gls{zlb} and \gls{zlb} periods and $0.27$ in the hiking cycle. This regularity reflects divergent communicative incentives: journalists are professionally rewarded for surfacing downside risks, while \gls{ecb} officials are constitutionally bound to project measured stability. The gap is a structural feature of the discourse, not a measurement error; had the pipeline not separated the two voices, the press corps' affective tone would have contaminated the policy signal by approximately $0.3$ on the unit scale.

\subsection{Does the Sectional Divergence Move Markets?}
\label{sec:divergence}

The two preceding subsections established that the IS and Q\&A carry structurally different information and that the journalist--\gls{ecb} gap is a stable institutional feature. A natural follow-up is whether either source of divergence carries market-moving content. Because three market variables are examined for each of two divergence measures, six Pearson correlations are tested simultaneously; we apply Holm--Bonferroni correction to the full family throughout and report both raw and adjusted $p$-values.\footnote{Under the global null, six independent tests at $\alpha = 0.05$ yield an expected false-positive count of $6 \times 0.05 = 0.30$ and a probability of at least one spurious significant result of $1 - (0.95)^{6} \approx 26\%$. The Holm--Bonferroni procedure controls the family-wise error rate while being uniformly more powerful than the plain Bonferroni correction \cite{holm1979}. Adjusted $p$-values are reproduced in the scatter-plot annotations of Figures~\ref{fig:divergence}--\ref{fig:gap} for transparency.}

Figure~\ref{fig:divergence} plots the absolute IS--Q\&A divergence against three market outcomes: the cross-maturity mean of $|\Delta\text{OIS}|$, the STOXX50 return, and the PC1 monetary surprise.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/is_qa/fig_divergence.pdf}
    \caption{%
        IS--Q\&A sentiment divergence versus market variables
        ($n = 272$ press conferences).
        Each panel reports Pearson $r$, raw $p$, and Holm-adjusted
        $p_{\mathrm{adj}}$; significance stars reflect $p_{\mathrm{adj}}$.
        Regression lines are shown for visual reference only.}
    \label{fig:divergence}
\end{figure}

The result is uniformly null after multiplicity correction. Neither the STOXX50 return ($r = 0.047$, $p_{\mathrm{adj}} = 1.000$) nor the PC1 monetary surprise ($r = -0.040$, $p_{\mathrm{adj}} = 1.000$) correlates meaningfully with the IS--Q\&A divergence. A nominally significant negative association emerges with $|\Delta\text{OIS}|$ ($r = -0.146$, $p_{\mathrm{raw}} = 0.016$), but this result does not survive Holm--Bonferroni correction ($p_{\mathrm{adj}} = 0.096$, Bonferroni threshold $\approx 0.0083$) and cannot be treated as a confirmed finding.

The \emph{direction} of this association is nonetheless theoretically interpretable: press conferences in which IS and Q\&A sentiment diverge most tend to produce the \emph{smallest} OIS reactions. We label this directional pattern \emph{communicative cancellation} --- the hypothesis that conflicting signals within the same press conference prevent the market from cleanly updating its rate expectations in either direction. We treat this as a motivated conjecture rather than an established result and revisit it in the regime-dependent rolling analysis of Section~\ref{sec:rolling}, where the same channel is examined conditional on the prevailing monetary policy uncertainty regime.

Figure~\ref{fig:gap} tests whether the journalist--\gls{ecb} sentiment gap within the Q\&A carries analogous market content.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/is_qa/fig_gap.pdf}
    \caption{%
        Internal Q--A sentiment gap versus market surprises
        ($n = 272$ press conferences).
        Annotation convention follows Figure~\ref{fig:divergence}.
        All three correlations are statistically indistinguishable from zero
        before and after family-wise correction.}
    \label{fig:gap}
\end{figure}

All three Q--A gap correlations are firmly null at both levels ($p_{\mathrm{adj}} \geq 0.764$ in every case; see Table~\ref{tab:holm_correction}). The near-flat regression lines in Figure~\ref{fig:gap} confirm the visual message. Markets appear to correctly anticipate the structural pessimism of the press corps and the measured caution of the \gls{ecb}; these expected components are priced immediately and leave no residual correlation with realised market moves. It is the \emph{deviation} from the institutionally expected baseline---not the level of the gap itself---that may carry market-moving content, a distinction testable only with event-study or high-frequency identification designs beyond the scope of this analysis.

In summary, neither the IS--Q\&A divergence nor the journalist--\gls{ecb} gap moves markets in a statistically confirmed manner once multiplicity is accounted for. The directional evidence for communicative cancellation on OIS rate expectations is suggestive and carries a clear economic interpretation, but it requires corroboration in a regime-conditional framework before it can support strong inference.
