\chapter{Data and Methodology}\label{ch:data}
\label{ch:methodology}
This chapter outlines the data-collection framework and the implementation of \gls{nlp} and machine-learning models. The data pipeline begins with the automated scraping and text processing of the \gls{ecb}'s monetary-policy statement transcripts. The extracted and processed text is then stored in a custom SQLite database, alongside sentiment and topic metadata produced by BERT-family models and a zero-shot topic classifier. This architecture ensures data integrity and provides a robust foundation for the subsequent econometric analysis of central bank communication against financial-market variables.

\section{Data Scraping and Preprocessing}
\label{sec:scraping}

Although datasets of \gls{ecb} communications are publicly available, most sources provide uniform text blocks that do not distinguish the \gls{is} from the \gls{qa} session, which limits their value for fine-grained sentiment analysis. A custom scraping pipeline is therefore required to isolate the responses of \gls{ecb} officials from journalists' questions and extract
\gls{mp} signals free from the sentiment bias of journalistic questioning. The first phase of the pipeline retrieves the transcript hyperlinks from
\gls{gc} press conferences and parses their content into two logical segments stored in a structured format.

After downloading the raw HTML, the pipeline separates the \gls{is} from the
\gls{qa} session using custom regular expressions that target structural markers such as \textbf{``My first question\ldots''} or \textbf{``Transcript of the questions''}. Within the \gls{qa} segment, the same mechanism distinguishes the responses of \gls{ecb} officials from the journalists' questions. The extracted text, together with dates and source URLs, is temporarily stored in Pipe-Separated Values (PSV) format.

In total, the pipeline retrieves \textbf{289 transcripts}, covering all press conferences between June 1998 and October 2024.\footnote{\nolinkurl{https://www.ecb.europa.eu/press/press_conference/monetary-policy-statement/html/index.en.html}} All transcripts share the same HTML layout, with content concentrated in \texttt{<p>} tags and headings of varying levels. The parsed output forms our \textbf{base dataset}, subsequently refined using the \texttt{nltk}, \texttt{re}, and \texttt{pandas} Python libraries.

The structural separation alone does not guarantee a clean signal: ceremonial greetings (e.g., \textit{``Good afternoon\ldots''}), procedural transitions, and short conversational fillers (e.g., \textit{``Yes.''}) carry no \gls{mp} information yet distort the sentiment baseline. To address this, the cleaning pipeline combines two filters: a Regex matcher that removes a list of pre-defined sentences, and a length-based heuristic that discards paragraphs shorter than $100$ characters. The latter effectively eliminates section headers and short fillers without affecting substantive policy statements. Illustrative cases are presented in Table~\ref{tab:redundant}.

\subsection{Splitting the Q\&A Section into Questions and Answers}
\label{sec:data_qa_construction}

A second structural challenge arises within the \gls{qa} segment itself: unlike the continuous \gls{is} monologue, the dialogue between journalists and the \gls{ecb} President requires explicit speaker attribution, which the HTML mark-up does not provide consistently. At least three distinct conventions for distinguishing questions from answers coexist in the corpus and evolve over time, as illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:qa_styling}.

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{images/data/qa_styling.png}
    \caption[Evolution of formatting styles in ECB transcripts]{Evolution of formatting styles in \gls{ecb} transcripts. The 2005 transcript (right) uses explicit speaker labels, whereas the 2024 version (left) relies on font styling (bold/italic) to distinguish between speakers.} \label{fig:qa_styling}
\end{figure}

Since each question and each answer occupies its own paragraph, Q\&A turns are stored row-wise in the PSV file introduced in Section~\ref{sec:scraping}. The resulting dataset has five primary features: \textbf{statement\_id} (unique identifier), \textbf{date}, \textbf{url}, \textbf{intro} (the prepared introductory statement), and \textbf{qa} (the unscripted dialogue).

Within each row, the \textit{intro} and \textit{qa} fields are tab-delimited at paragraph level to preserve the internal structure of the conference. The \textit{qa} field additionally wraps text segments in square brackets so as to retain the original bold and italic markers, which the \gls{ecb} employs to flag a journalist's question against the President's answer (Figure~\ref{fig:qa_styling}). This convention is not universal: some transcripts instead prefix each turn with an explicit label such as \texttt{Question:} or the speaker's name, followed by unformatted text.

\label{sec:quantifying}

Transforming qualitative narratives into quantitative market predictors requires a systematic refinement of the base dataset assembled in Section~\ref{sec:scraping}. The pipeline introduced in this section first segments each transcript into model-ready chunks and then, in parallel, applies zero-shot topic classification and sentiment scoring before persisting both the standardised chunks and their derived scores in an SQLite database. The overall architecture is illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:data_pipeline_snake}; its output consists of two specialised collections corresponding to the \gls{is} and \gls{qa} components, each preserving an audit trail back to every raw HTML paragraph.

\subsection{Text Segmentation}
\label{sec:segmentation}

Sentiment classifiers operate on individual chunks rather than entire documents, and the BERT-based architectures employed in this work impose a hard 512-token input limit. To satisfy both constraints while preserving argumentative context, we set the chunk length to $200$ words (${\approx}260$--$340$ subword tokens for typical central-bank prose). Paragraphs exceeding this limit are split at sentence boundaries using \texttt{nltk}'s sentence-boundary detector, whereas paragraphs shorter than $30$ words are kept as single segments without further splitting. Each segment inherits its parent's metadata (date, speaker, section type). The resulting word-count distribution, with both thresholds explicitly marked, is shown in Figure~\ref{fig:distribution}.

\begin{figure}[ht]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{images/data/segment_distribution.pdf}
    \caption[Impact of segmentation on text length distribution]{Impact
        of segmentation on text length distribution.}
    \label{fig:distribution}
\end{figure}

The $200$-word threshold is the outcome of a sensitivity analysis across eight granularities ($1$, $50$, $100$, $150$, $200$, $250$, $300$, $350$ words), reported in Table~\ref{tab:chunk-sensitivity}. The Pearson correlation between the FinBERT and CentralBankRoBERTa meeting-level means declines monotonically from $r = 0.727$ at single-sentence level to $r = 0.589$ at $350$ words, while the within-meeting standard deviation $\bar{\sigma}$ stabilises beyond $200$ words ($\Delta\bar{\sigma} < 0.001$ thereafter). Single-sentence chunks therefore maximise cross-model agreement but fragment multi-sentence economic arguments; $200$ words is selected as the operating point that balances signal stability against context preservation. Granularities of $1$ and $50$ words are revisited as robustness checks in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.

\subsection{Zero-Shot Topic Classification}
\label{sec:topic_classification}

The segmented chunks are now structurally clean but remain unlabelled. Traditional dictionary-based approaches cannot handle context such as negations or complex sentence patterns, and training a domain-specific topic-modelling transformer was infeasible given the absence of a pre-labelled \gls{ecb} corpus and the time constraints of this project.

We therefore adopt \textbf{zero-shot classification}, which assigns arbitrary text to a pre-defined set of labels without label-specific training. The chosen backbone is \emph{facebook/bart-large-mnli}, pre-trained on \gls{nli}: each thematic label is treated as a hypothesis and the model returns its entailment probability against the input segment.

    \label{tab:labels}
    \begin{tabular}{@{}lp{8cm}@{}}
        \toprule
        \textbf{Category}    & \textbf{Model Input (Label String)}                                                                                                            \\ \midrule
        Monetary Policy      & ``inflation, price stability, interest rate decisions, monetary policy stance, financing conditions, bank lending, and market interest rates'' \\ \addlinespace
        Economic Performance & ``economic growth, GDP outlook, unemployment, labour market developments, macroeconomic risks, demand, consumption, and investment''           \\ \addlinespace
        Fiscal \& Structural & ``government budgets, national debt, public spending, and structural reforms''                                                                 \\ \addlinespace
        Other / Noise        & ``general greetings, purely political questions, unrelated remarks, climate change, or personal comments (excluding macroeconomic impacts)''   \\ \bottomrule
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

\paragraph{Iterating on the taxonomy.} An initial ten-label taxonomy caused the classifier to spread its probability mass thinly and collapse heterogeneous segments into a single dominant category. Reducing the taxonomy to four broader themes and replacing short titles with descriptive hypothesis strings (Table~\ref{tab:labels}) sharpened the entailment signal substantially. Because individual passages frequently touch on several themes at once (e.g., wage developments linked to the policy stance), the classifier is run in multi-label mode: every topic whose entailment probability exceeds $\tau = 0.45$ is assigned to the chunk, and the top-1 label is retained as a fallback when no label clears the threshold. The \emph{Other} category isolates thematic noise---procedural exchanges and remarks without market-moving potential---so that its downstream effect on the sentiment signal can be tested explicitly in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.

\paragraph{Robustness with a second NLI backbone.} To rule out a model-specific artefact, the pipeline is replicated with a second backbone, \emph{MoritzLaurer/DeBERTa-v3-large-mnli-fever-anli-ling-wanli} \cite{laurer2024deberta}, which differs from BART-MNLI both architecturally (encoder-only, disentangled attention) and in its substantially broader \gls{nli} fine-tuning corpus (MultiNLI, FEVER-NLI, ANLI, LingNLI, WANLI). Both backbones are applied to identical chunks under the same four-topic taxonomy and entailment threshold.

At the primary 200-word chunk size, top-1 agreement between the two backbones reaches only $\kappa = 0.155$ (raw agreement $27.8\,\%$, $n = 18{,}557$ chunks) \cite{landis1977measurement}. DeBERTa-v3 systematically routes $60$--$72\,\%$ of substantive \gls{ecb} passages to the \textsc{Other\,/\,Irrelevant} class---a known consequence of its adversarial \gls{nli} fine-tuning raising the entailment bar beyond what our hypothesis strings can clear \cite{laurer2024deberta}. The comparison therefore does not constitute an independent robustness check: all downstream analyses use BART-large-mnli labels exclusively, and the DeBERTa results are reported as a transparency note.

\subsection{Multi-Model Sentiment Extraction}
\label{sec:sentiment_extraction}

With each chunk labelled, the next stage of the pipeline quantifies its tone. To guard against the idiosyncrasies of any single model, sentiment is extracted in parallel by two pre-trained transformers: \textbf{FinBERT} \cite{araci2019finbert}---a BERT-base encoder pre-trained on Reuters TRC2 and fine-tuned on the Financial PhraseBank--- and \textbf{CentralBankRoBERTa} \cite{Pfeifer2023}, a RoBERTa-base encoder fine-tuned on a curated corpus of central-bank speeches and reports. Both return a binary positive/negative polarity in the sense of Equation~\ref{eq:sentiment}, but differ in encoder family, tokeniser, and fine-tuning data, providing two largely independent readings of the same text. Agreement on the sign of a chunk's sentiment can therefore be treated as a model-invariant signal, whereas disagreement is itself informative and is analysed in Chapter~\ref{ch:results}. Representative outputs are shown in Table~\ref{tab:sentiment_examples}.

\section{Database Architecture}
\label{sec:db}

All artefacts produced by the pipeline---raw chunks, sentiment scores, and topic labels---are persisted in a custom \emph{SQLite} database. The choice is deliberate: the dataset fits comfortably on a single machine, while the relational schema enforces the referential constraints that make every score and every label reproducibly traceable to a single original press conference.

The schema, illustrated in Figure~\ref{fig:db_schema}, consists of seven normalised tables. The design separates raw text (\texttt{chunks}), model registries (\texttt{sentiment\_models}, \texttt{topic\_models}), the taxonomy (\texttt{topic\_labels}), and the model-scored relations (\texttt{sentiments}, \texttt{topics}). Two design choices deserve emphasis. First, \texttt{topics} stores $(\texttt{chunk}, \texttt{label}, \texttt{model})$ triples together with the raw entailment probability \texttt{prob}, so that the multi-label threshold $\tau$ can be re-tuned post hoc without re-running the classifier and the BART/DeBERTa comparison of Section~\ref{sec:topic_classification} can be performed against a common chunk store. Second, the \texttt{chunk\_limit} column on \texttt{chunks} permits the simultaneous storage of multiple segmentation granularities, so that the chunk-size sensitivity analysis of Section~\ref{sec:segmentation} runs against a single, internally consistent backing store.

\section{Financial Market Data}
\label{sec:market_data}

To evaluate the informational value of the textual signal, the latter is confronted with a panel of financial indicators organised in three layers: high-frequency market surprises, official policy rates, and a derived structural shock.

\paragraph{High-frequency market surprises.} The primary source of market expectations is the Euro Area Monetary Policy Event-Study Database (EA-MPD) of \cite{altavilla2019measuring}, from which we extract intraday changes in \gls{ois} rates at the $1$-month, $3$-month, $6$-month, and $1$-year maturities. The narrow event window around each press conference filters out unrelated macroeconomic news and thereby isolates the surprise component of \gls{ecb} communication. The corresponding intraday change in the STOXX50 equity index is added to capture the broader risk reaction.

\paragraph{Official policy rates.} The \gls{mro} rate and the Deposit Facility (DF) rate, sourced from the
\gls{ecb} money-market archives, represent the explicit policy signal. For periods in which nominal rates are constrained by the \gls{zlb}, the Wu--Xia shadow rate \cite{wu2016measuring} is used as a proxy for unconstrained monetary conditions.

\paragraph{First principal component of the OIS surprises.} To collapse the mul\-ti-di\-men\-sional \gls{ois} surprise vector onto a single target variable, we extract its first principal component, denoted \textbf{PC1}. PC1 captures the dominant level shift across the maturity spectrum and serves as a parsimonious summary of the policy surprise for the predictive analysis of Chapter~\ref{ch:results}.

The three layers are joined to the meeting-level feature matrix of Section~\ref{sec:feature_construction} on the press-conference date, yielding the unified $(\text{date} \times \text{feature})$ panel on which the empirical analysis operates.

\section{Data Integration and Modular Feature Framework}
\label{sec:feature_construction}

The final stage of the methodology integrates the textual and financial data into a single feature matrix. Rather than committing to a fixed table layout, the pipeline is implemented as a \textbf{modular framework} whose granularity is selected at run time, so that the same codebase supports hypotheses ranging from aggregate market reactions to the granular dialogue between journalists and the central bank.

\subsection{Configurable Granularities}
\label{sec:granularities}

The framework exposes three nested levels of granularity. The
\textbf{Global} configuration treats each transcript as a single unit, collapsing all chunks into one sentiment summary per meeting. The
\textbf{Sectional} configuration splits the discourse into the prepared
\gls{is} and the spontaneous \gls{qa} session, isolating the prepared narrative from the live exchange. The \textbf{Thematic} configuration additionally cross-cuts the data with the four macroeconomic topic labels of Section~\ref{sec:topic_classification}, producing a multi-dimensional view of the President's stance on each policy area. A finer fourth mode separates \textbf{journalist questions from official answers}, providing the substrate for the divergence analysis of Section~\ref{sec:divergence}.

\subsection{The Chunk-to-Meeting Aggregation Operator}
\label{sec:aggregation}

The relational database of Section~\ref{sec:db} stores sentiment and topic outputs at the granularity of the individual chunk, whereas econometric analysis requires a feature matrix indexed by press-conference date. Let $\mathcal{C}_{t,m,s,\tau}$ denote the set of chunks observed at press conference $t$, scored by sentiment model $m \in \{\text{FinBERT}, \text{CB-RoBERTa}\}$, belonging to section $s \in \{\text{IS}, \text{QA}\}$, and classified under topic $\tau \in \{\text{MP}, \text{EP}, \text{FS}, \text{OI}\}$. For each non-empty cell, the chunk-level scores are summarised by the four-dimensional moment vector

\begin{equation}
    \label{eq:aggregation}
    \mathbf{f}_{t,m,s,\tau}
    \;=\;
    \bigl(\overline{S}_{t,m,s,\tau},\; \sigma_{t,m,s,\tau},\; S^{\min}_{t,m,s,\tau},\; S^{\max}_{t,m,s,\tau}\bigr).
\end{equation}

The mean captures the central tendency of the tone, the standard deviation captures within-meeting dispersion, and the extrema preserve the strongest hawkish and dovish signals without requiring the analyst to specify a quantile threshold ex ante. The vector is computed across every non-empty cell of the four-way product $\{t\} \times \{m\} \times \{s\} \times \{\tau\}$, yielding a sparse but information-rich representation of each press conference.

\paragraph{Treatment of the Q\&A section.} Within \gls{qa} cells, aggregation is restricted to the President's answers: chunks flagged as journalist questions (\texttt{is\_question = 1}) are excluded before Equation~\ref{eq:aggregation} is applied, so that the tone of the press corps does not contaminate the policy signal. The excluded journalist chunks are retained in the database and revisited in Section~\ref{sec:divergence}, where the gap between journalist framing and the \gls{ecb}'s answers is treated as an object of analysis in its own right.

\subsection{Missing Cells and Final Merge}
\label{sec:final_merge}

The Cartesian product $\{t\} \times \{m\} \times \{s\} \times \{\tau\}$ contains cells for which $\mathcal{C}_{t,m,s,\tau}$ is empty---most commonly when a routine conference, dominated by inflation discussion, contains no chunks classified as \texttt{FISCAL\_AND\_STRUCTURAL}. To avoid the cross-sectional leakage that column-mean imputation would introduce, each missing entry is instead filled by carrying the most recent non-missing value of the same series forward in time. This encodes the economically natural prior that the silence of the central bank on a topic does not reset market beliefs about its most recently communicated tone on that topic. Observations preceding the first non-missing value of any series are excluded from the analysis sample, as no carry-forward is feasible without prior information.

The meeting-level feature matrix is then inner-joined on the press-conference date with the financial-market panel introduced in Section~\ref{sec:market_data}; meetings lacking either the textual record or the high-frequency market window are dropped at this stage. The surviving sample constitutes the unified $(\text{date} \times \text{feature})$ matrix on which the empirical analysis of Chapter~\ref{ch:results} operates. That chapter subjects these signals to four progressively more demanding tests: their internal coherence (Sections~\ref{sec:results_sentiment}--\ref{sec:results_validation}), their sectional and thematic structure (Section~\ref{sec:results_sectional}), their co-movement with the monetary-policy cycle (Section~\ref{sec:results_cycle}), and their predictive value for the direction of \gls{ecb} rate decisions (Section~\ref{sec:predictive}).
