\chapter{Results}\label{ch:results}

This chapter reports the empirical findings of the thesis. The analysis proceeds in four arcs of increasing demand on the data. Section~\ref{sec:results_sentiment} establishes that the two sentiment classifiers produce internally coherent signals and shows that those signals track major historical episodes of monetary-policy stress. Section~\ref{sec:results_sectional} dissects the press conference into its prepared and spontaneous components, demonstrating that the \gls{is} and the \gls{qa} session carry structurally different information. Section~\ref{sec:results_cycle} relates the aggregate sentiment series to the \gls{ecb}'s policy-rate cycle, including an event-study of rate decisions. Section~\ref{sec:predictive} closes the loop by asking whether the textual signal carries predictive value for the direction of the next rate decision, comparing four classifiers of increasing complexity (a logit baseline, a sentiment-only logit, an augmented logit, and a random forest) and identifying the regimes in which sentiment-derived features add information beyond the rate history alone.

\section{Aggregate Sentiment and Model Comparison}
\label{sec:results_sentiment}

Before exploring the granular structure of \gls{ecb} communication, we must confirm a more basic requirement: that the two sentiment classifiers produce signals that are internally coherent and behave as central-bank discourse should behave. Figure~\ref{fig:aggregate_sentiment} plots the six-month centred moving average of both models against time, together with $\pm 1$ standard-deviation bands constructed from the chunk-level distributions within each meeting.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/base/fig_aggregate_sentiment.pdf}
    \caption[Aggregate ECB press-conference sentiment, 1999--2024]{Aggregate \gls{ecb} press-conference sentiment, 1999--2024. Solid lines show the six-month centred moving average; shaded bands show the within-meeting standard deviation. Blue: FinBERT; dark red: CentralBankRoBERTa.}
    \label{fig:aggregate_sentiment}
\end{figure}

A persistent vertical gap separates the two series throughout the sample. FinBERT remains in mildly positive territory, while CentralBankRoBERTa is anchored below zero. The gap reflects neither a malfunction of the pipeline nor a disagreement about the direction of policy: it is a calibration difference inherited from the respective fine-tuning corpora. FinBERT was trained on Reuters financial news, where vocabulary such as \textit{``growth''} or \textit{``profit''} is over-represented and carries a positive prior, whereas CentralBankRoBERTa was fine-tuned on central-bank speeches and reports, where the institutional baseline is one of measured caution. What is identical across the two series is the \emph{shape} of the trajectory: troughs around the dot-com episode, the Global Financial Crisis, the sovereign-debt crisis, and 2020 appear in both. \section{Sanity Check: Validation against Historical Events}
\label{sec:results_validation}

A correlated pair of series can still be jointly wrong. To verify that the signal aligns with the policy record, Figure~\ref{fig:sentiment_events} overlays the aggregate trajectory with five anchor points: the October 2008 emergency rate cut, Draghi's ``Whatever it takes'' speech of July 2012, the launch of the asset-purchase programme in January 2015, the COVID-emergency response in March 2020, and the start of the post-pandemic hiking cycle in July 2022.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/base/fig_sentiment_events.pdf}
    \caption[ECB sentiment and key historical policy events]{Aggregate \gls{ecb} sentiment overlaid with five anchor events. Dotted vertical lines mark the event date.}
    \label{fig:sentiment_events}

The sentiment series turn down sharply at the GFC anchor, recover during the ``Whatever it takes'' window, drift downward through the~protracted \gls{zlb} accommodation, dip again in 2020, and turn unambiguously upward at the onset of the 2022 hiking cycle. \section{The Sectional Anatomy of Policy Communication}
\label{sec:results_sectional}

The headline series collapses the rich internal structure of a press conference into a single number. We now exploit the sectional split documented in Section~\ref{sec:data_qa_construction} to ask whether the prepared and spontaneous components communicate different information.

\subsection{IS versus Q\&A: Two Different Documents}
\label{sec:results_is_vs_qa}

Figure~\ref{fig:IS_vs_QA} plots the two sections separately across the full sample. Three regularities are immediately visible. First, the IS sentiment dominates the QA sentiment at almost every date. Second, the IS series is markedly more volatile than the QA series. Third, the two series move broadly in the same direction at low frequency but diverge sharply at higher frequencies.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/is_qa/fig_IS_vs_QA.pdf}
    \caption[Prepared versus spontaneous communication]{Introductory Statement versus Q\&A sentiment over time, FinBERT (left) and CentralBankRoBERTa (right).}
    \label{fig:IS_vs_QA}
\end{figure}

The scatter in Figure~\ref{fig:Scatter_IS_vs_QA} confirms the structural offset: the bulk of the cloud sits below the 45-degree line, i.e. the live dialogue is almost always more negative than the prepared text. The correlation is positive but the slope is well below unity. The two sections share less than $10\%$ of their variance, meaning the bulk of the \gls{qa}'s tone is information that is simply not present in the prepared statement.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/is_qa/fig_Scatter_IS_vs_QA.pdf}
    \caption[IS--Q\&A scatter, coloured by date]{IS--Q\&A sentiment scatter. Points are colour-coded by date. The dashed line marks perfect agreement.}
    \label{fig:Scatter_IS_vs_QA}
\end{figure}

\subsection{The Internal Story Arc}
\label{sec:results_arc}

Because the data is segmented into short chunks, we can also track how sentiment evolves \emph{within} each section. Figure~\ref{fig:intra_section} bins the chunks by their relative position within each section---from the first chunk to the last---and averages across all $272$ conferences.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/is_qa/fig_intra_section_both.pdf}
    \caption[Intra-section sentiment trajectory]{Intra-section sentiment trajectory. Left: the \gls{is} dips as economic risks are catalogued, then rises as policy decisions are announced. Right: the \gls{qa} shows no trajectory; \gls{ecb} answers (red/dark red) score consistently higher than journalist questions (orange). Shaded bands: $\pm 1$ standard error.}
    \label{fig:intra_section}
\end{figure}

The \gls{is} profile traces a coherent three-act structure. It opens at a moderate level (the economic-context paragraphs), dips between the one-fifth and the two-fifths marks (where the bank traditionally catalogues downside risks), and rises sharply through the final passages (where the actual policy decision and forward guidance are stated in decisive language). The \gls{qa} profile, by contrast, is essentially flat: a sharp question can land in the first minute as easily as in the last, and treating the \gls{qa} as a single narrative document averages over a random sequence of topics rather than a coherent argumentative arc. This is the empirical justification for the chunk-level aggregation operator of Section~\ref{sec:aggregation}.

\subsection{The Journalist--ECB Sentiment Gap}
\label{sec:results_gap}

When the \gls{qa} session is decomposed further into journalist questions and \gls{ecb} answers, a second structural regularity emerges. Figure~\ref{fig:QA_timeseries} plots the two voices separately. The journalist line sits a stable distance below the \gls{ecb} line, with the gap remaining remarkably stable across structurally different monetary regimes.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/is_qa/fig_QA_timeseries.pdf}
    \caption[Journalist versus ECB sentiment over time]{Journalist questions versus \gls{ecb} answers, CentralBankRoBERTa, six-month centred moving average.}
    \label{fig:QA_timeseries}
\end{figure}

Figure~\ref{fig:QA_distribution} characterises the gap quantitatively. The signed gap $S_Q - S_A$ has a mean of $-0.293$ and a median of $-0.248$, and journalists are more dovish than the \gls{ecb} in $78\%$ of question--answer pairs. The right panel splits the absolute gap by era and shows that the average size of the gap is roughly constant across the Pre-\gls{zlb}, \gls{zlb}, and Hiking regimes.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/is_qa/fig_QA_distribution.pdf}
    \caption[Distribution of the Q--A sentiment gap]{Distribution of the journalist-minus-\gls{ecb} sentiment gap. Left: full-sample histogram. Right: absolute gap by monetary regime.}
    \label{fig:QA_distribution}
\end{figure}

This regularity reflects the divergent communicative incentives of the two parties. Financial journalists are professionally rewarded for surfacing downside risks, while central bank officials are constitutionally bound to project measured stability. The gap is therefore not a measurement error but a structural feature of the discourse; importantly, had the pipeline not separated the two voices, the affective tone of the press corps would have contaminated the policy signal by an order of $0.3$ on the unit scale.

\subsection{Does the IS--Q\&A Divergence Move Markets?}
\label{sec:divergence}

Given the tonal split between the prepared and the spontaneous sections, a natural question is whether the gap between them carries informational value for the high-frequency market variables. Figure~\ref{fig:divergence} regresses three market outcomes---the cross-maturity mean of $|\Delta\text{OIS}|$, the STOXX50 return, and the PC1 monetary surprise---on the absolute \gls{is}--\gls{qa} sentiment divergence.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/is_qa/fig_divergence.pdf}
    \caption[IS--Q\&A divergence versus market variables]{IS--Q\&A communication divergence versus market variables (CentralBankRoBERTa). A larger disagreement between the speech and the \gls{qa} is associated with slightly \emph{smaller} OIS reactions ($r = -0.145^{*}$, left). Equity returns and PC1 monetary surprise show no significant relationship. $n = 272$.}
    \label{fig:divergence}
\end{figure}

The result is largely null, with one counter-intuitive exception. Neither the STOXX50 return nor the PC1 monetary surprise correlates significantly with the divergence. However, a small but statistically significant \emph{negative} correlation is observed with the cross-maturity mean of $|\Delta\text{OIS}|$ ($r = -0.145$, $p < 0.05$): conferences where the two sections diverge the most are associated with \emph{smaller} OIS reactions, not larger ones. A plausible mechanism is that when the \gls{is} is hawkish but the \gls{qa} turns dovish, the market receives conflicting signals within the same hour and cannot cleanly update its rate expectations in either direction. We refer to this regularity as \emph{communicative cancellation}: high internal tension yields a muted external impact.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/is_qa/fig_gap.pdf}
    \caption[Internal Q--A gap versus market surprises]{The internal Q--A gap (journalist question minus \gls{ecb} answer) plotted against OIS changes ($r = -0.048$, n.s.) and PC1 monetary surprise ($r = -0.003$, n.s.). The structural journalist--\gls{ecb} gap is orthogonal to market surprises. $n = 272$.}
    \label{fig:gap}
\end{figure}

The within-\gls{qa} gap between journalist questions and \gls{ecb} answers (Figure~\ref{fig:gap}) tells the complementary story: the correlations with both OIS changes and PC1 surprises are statistically indistinguishable from zero. Markets correctly anticipate the structural pessimism of journalists and the structural caution of central bankers, and these expected components are immediately discounted. It is the \emph{deviation} from the institutionally expected baseline---not the level of the gap---that carries the surprise.

In summary, the sectional analysis validates the granular pipeline. The \gls{is} is a structured narrative in which the policy-specific chunks at the end carry the strongest market signal; the \gls{qa} is a flat, reactive dialogue divided between a journalistic framing of risk and an institutional response. Treating the press conference as a single document conflates fundamentally different communicative acts and discards the hidden informational value of each section.

\section{Sentiment and the Monetary Policy Cycle}
\label{sec:results_cycle}

The previous sections established that our sentiment indices are internally coherent and reflect major historical turning points. A more demanding test is whether they bear a systematic, quantifiable relationship to the actual \emph{price of money}: the \gls{ecb}'s policy interest rates. If the words in an \gls{ecb} press conference genuinely encode monetary-policy intentions, then hawkish language should accompany high or rising rates, and dovish language should precede or coincide with rate cuts.

\subsection{Co-Movement with Policy Rates}
\label{sec:results_comovement}

Figure~\ref{fig:sentiment_vs_rates} plots the smoothed \gls{ecb} sentiment series against the \gls{mro} rate and, in the \gls{zlb} window, against the Wu--Xia shadow rate as an alternative monetary-conditions proxy \footnote{The Wu--Xia shadow rate \cite{wu2016measuring} recovers a notional policy rate that would have produced the same long-rate dynamics as the observed combination of forward guidance and quantitative easing, allowing comparison of policy stances across \gls{zlb} and non-\gls{zlb} episodes.}.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/mro/fig_sentiment_vs_rates.pdf}
    \caption[Sentiment versus policy rates]{Sentiment (CentralBankRoBERTa, six-month centred moving average) versus the \gls{mro} rate and the Wu--Xia shadow rate. The shaded period corresponds to the \gls{zlb} era during which the \gls{mro} was constrained at or below zero.}
    \label{fig:sentiment_vs_rates}
\end{figure}

Three regimes are visible. In the pre-\gls{zlb} window (1999--2011), sentiment and the \gls{mro} rate move broadly in tandem, with sentiment leading the rate adjustments by a small number of meetings. In the \gls{zlb} window (2012--2021), the \gls{mro} flatlines near zero, while the shadow rate continues to decline towards approximately $-8\%$ by 2020---reflecting the cumulative effect of forward guidance and asset purchases. The sentiment series does \emph{not} track this descent: it remains close to zero through the entire \gls{zlb} accommodation. This is not a failure of the pipeline. The hawk-dove vocabulary on which the classifiers were trained refers to the language of orthodox rate changes, and the language of quantitative easing---\textit{``purchase programme''}, \textit{``reinvestment''}, \textit{``favourable financing conditions''}---is structurally different. The mismatch between the \gls{ecb}'s rhetorical toolkit and the classifier vocabulary is a property of the underlying training distribution, not of the implementation. We return to this limitation in Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion}. In the post-\gls{zlb} hiking window (2022--2024), sentiment turns sharply positive, comovement with the rate is restored, and the lead-lag structure re-emerges.

\subsection{The Anatomy of Tightening versus Easing: An Event Study}
\label{sec:event_study}

The aggregate co-movement raises a sharper question: does the \gls{ecb} telegraph its rate changes \emph{before} they happen, and does it communicate differently when preparing to tighten than when preparing to ease? To answer this we adopt a standard event-study design. We align all historical rate decisions so that the meeting of the rate change is day zero (lag $= 0$), and we extract the sentiment trajectory in a $\pm 6$-meeting window around the event. The analysis is restricted to the conventional pre-\gls{zlb} period (1999--2013) and the recent post-\gls{zlb} period (2021--2025); the \gls{zlb} era itself is excluded because rate changes are too rare to support meaningful window averages.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/mro/fig_event_study.pdf}
    \caption[Event study: sentiment around ECB rate decisions]{Event study: sentiment trajectory in a $\pm 6$-meeting window around \gls{ecb} rate decisions. Red lines represent rate hikes; blue lines represent rate cuts. Lag $= 0$ is the meeting at which the rate is changed. Left panel: the pre-\gls{zlb} era. Right panel: the post-\gls{zlb} era. Shaded bands show $\pm 1$ standard error.}
    \label{fig:event_study}
\end{figure}

The pre-\gls{zlb} panel of Figure~\ref{fig:event_study} shows a striking asymmetry between the two trajectories. The hawkish profile (red) is fully established six meetings before the actual hike, forming a high, flat plateau---the \gls{ecb} adopts a hawkish tone and maintains it for many months before raising the rate. The dovish profile (blue) is qualitatively different: sentiment remains near zero until one or two meetings before the cut, when it dives sharply into negative territory. Historically, rate cuts in this period were predominantly emergency reactions to negative shocks, most notably the 2008 Global Financial Crisis, and the central bank did not have the luxury of a six-month preparation window. After the cut, sentiment recovers rapidly to zero as the \gls{ecb} frames the action as a stabilising response.

The post-\gls{zlb} panel reveals a completely different communication regime. The two trajectories now form a near-symmetric X-shape, crossing close to the moment of the decision. In this regime, \emph{both} hikes and cuts are heavily telegraphed: when preparing for the 2022--2023 hikes, the \gls{ecb} starts with strongly hawkish language at lag $= -6$ that softens as the hike approaches; when preparing for the subsequent cuts, sentiment starts deeply negative and gradually improves. After the decision, the trajectories diverge again, with sentiment moving in the direction opposite to the decision: once hiking begins, the bank emphasises caution about over-tightening; once cutting begins, the bank emphasises confidence that inflation has been brought under control.

The headline implication is that sentiment \emph{leads} the policy action and that the lead is structurally different between hiking and easing. This already suggests that the textual signal carries information beyond what is contained in the policy rate itself, motivating the formal predictive analysis of Section~\ref{sec:predictive}.

\section{Predictive Value of Sentiment}
\label{sec:predictive}

The preceding sections have demonstrated that the sentiment series is coherent, that it reflects historical episodes, that it differs systematically between the prepared and spontaneous sections of the press conference, and that it leads the policy rate around decision events. We now ask the operational question that follows from these descriptive results: \emph{does the textual signal predict the direction of the next ECB rate decision once we control for the rate history itself?} We address it in three steps. Section~\ref{sec:results_correlations} characterises the predictive correlation between sentiment features and rate changes across monetary regimes and lag horizons. Section~\ref{sec:results_prediction} fits four classifiers of increasing complexity---a logit baseline, a sentiment-only logit, an augmented logit, and a random-forest extension---to the meeting-level rate-decision direction. Section~\ref{sec:results_surprise} closes the chapter with a brief robustness check on communication surprise and market volatility.

\subsection{Cross-Era Predictive Correlations}
\label{sec:results_correlations}

The lag-correlation analysis in Figure~\ref{fig:lag_profile} traces, for the three highest-information sentiment features, the Pearson correlation between the lagged feature and the contemporaneous change in the \gls{mro} rate (filtered to changed meetings only) as a function of the lag horizon in meetings. Two patterns emerge. First, the correlation is uniformly negligible at lag $0$ and rises monotonically through the first few lags, indicating that the textual signal carries information that the contemporaneous rate change does not. Second, the lag at which the correlation peaks differs by feature: the FinBERT overall mean peaks at lag $5$ ($r = 0.565$), the FinBERT \gls{is}-restricted mean peaks at lag $2$ ($r = 0.315$), and the CentralBankRoBERTa \gls{is} mean peaks at lag $10$ ($r = 0.380$). The textual signal is therefore not a short-horizon nowcast: it leads rate changes by between two and ten meetings, depending on which slice of the discourse is used.

In the \gls{zlb} era the target is replaced by the change in the Wu--Xia shadow rate, because the \gls{mro} rate carries essentially no variation in this window.

\subsection{Predicting the Direction of ECB Rate Decisions}
\label{sec:results_prediction}

The predictive task is operationalised as a three-class classification problem. Each press conference is assigned a label $y_t \in \{-1, 0, +1\}$ corresponding to the sign of the change in the \gls{mro} rate at that meeting: $-1$ if the rate is cut, $0$ if held, and $+1$ if hiked. The class distribution is heavily skewed towards \emph{hold} ($79.2\%$ of meetings), which sets a high naive baseline: a classifier that always predicts \emph{hold} achieves $79.2\%$ accuracy.

Four classifiers are compared:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{M1 (Baseline)}: a logistic regression on $\{\text{mro\_lag},\, \text{mro\_change\_lag}\}$. This is the rate-history-only benchmark.
    \item \textbf{M2 (Sentiment)}: a logistic regression on four sentiment features. FinBERT overall mean at lag $2$, FinBERT overall mean at lag $5$, FinBERT \gls{is} mean at lag $2$, and the FinBERT \gls{is} standard deviation. This isolates the marginal informational content of the textual signal.
    \item \textbf{M3 (Augmented)}: a logistic regression on the union of M1 and M2 features. This tests whether sentiment adds information beyond the rate history.
    \item \textbf{M4 (Random Forest)}: a random-forest classifier on the M3 feature set. This relaxes the linearity assumption and allows the model to capture interactions between rate history and sentiment.
\end{itemize}

All models are fitted with class-balanced loss weights and evaluated by $5$-fold time-series cross-validation, which respects the temporal ordering of the meetings and never trains on future data. The performance metrics are the cross-validated accuracy (point estimate $\pm$ across-fold standard deviation) and the multi-class area under the receiver-operating curve (AUC, one-vs-rest). Figure~\ref{fig:performance} reports both metrics for the four models; the dashed grey line marks the naive baseline.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.95\linewidth]{images/results/model/fig_performance.pdf}
    \caption[Cross-validated performance of the four predictive models]{Cross-validated performance of the four predictive models. Solid bars: accuracy; outlined bars: AUC (one-vs-rest). Error bars on the accuracy bars show the standard deviation across folds. The dashed line marks the naive baseline of always predicting \textit{hold}.}
    \label{fig:performance}
\end{figure}

Three observations follow. First, the accuracy of the sentiment-only model (M2) is below the naive baseline. This is the expected outcome of a class-balanced classifier on a heavily imbalanced sample: M2 makes positive predictions for the minority classes (\textit{hike} and \textit{cut}) and is consequently penalised on accuracy whenever those predictions are wrong, even when the model identifies the correct direction. Accuracy therefore systematically understates the value of M2 on this dataset, which is why we read the comparison primarily through the AUC. Second, the AUC ordering is M1 $<$ M2 $<$ M3 $<$ M4. Adding sentiment to the rate-history baseline lifts AUC from approximately $0.68$ (M1) to $0.72$ (M3). The random-forest extension (M4) provides a further modest gain, consistent with the presence of mild non-linearities and interactions between rate history and sentiment that the logistic specification cannot capture. Third, the gap between M3 and M2 is informative: the sentiment-only model does almost as well in terms of AUC as the augmented model, which means most of the predictive content captured by M3 is in the sentiment features rather than the rate-history features.

The confusion matrix in Figure~\ref{fig:confusion} disaggregates the M3 predictions by true class. The model identifies $25$ of $29$ cuts ($86\%$) and $22$ of $28$ hikes ($79\%$), but only $128$ of $218$ holds ($59\%$). The errors on the \emph{hold} class are not symmetric: $48$ holds are mistakenly classified as cuts and $42$ as hikes. In substantive terms, the model is reliable for the directional question (\textit{is the next move up or down?}) but less precise about the timing of the actual move within a sequence of holds---exactly the pattern one would expect from a sentiment signal that leads the rate change by several meetings (Section~\ref{sec:results_correlations}).

    \caption[Standardised coefficients of the augmented logistic model (M3)]{Standardised coefficients of the augmented logistic model (M3). Coefficients are reported for each of the three classes (cut, hold, hike) and each of the model's input features.}

The signed coefficients in Figure~\ref{fig:coefficients} confirm the economic intuition. The FinBERT overall mean at lag $5$ loads positively on the \textit{hike} class and negatively on the \textit{cut} class, i.e. hawkish language five meetings ago is associated with a higher probability of a hike today and a lower probability of a cut. The FinBERT \gls{is} mean at lag $2$ shows the same sign pattern but at a shorter horizon, consistent with the \gls{is}-led short-run channel identified in Section~\ref{sec:event_study}. The lagged \gls{mro} rate loads positively on \textit{hold}, capturing the institutional reality that the \gls{ecb} rarely reverses direction at consecutive meetings. The standard deviation of \gls{is} sentiment loads negatively on \textit{hold}, indicating that meetings preceded by more volatile communication are more likely to be followed by an actual decision rather than a continuation of the status quo.

\subsection{Communication Surprise and Market Volatility}
\label{sec:results_surprise}

The predictive analysis has so far focused on the level of sentiment and its lagged correlations with policy rates. A complementary perspective is to ask whether the \emph{change} in sentiment from one meeting to the next---the communication surprise---correlates with the contemporaneous market reaction during the press-conference window. Figure~\ref{fig:delta_sentiment} reports two such checks. The top row regresses the change in \gls{ois}-$1$Y on three lagged measures of $\Delta$sentiment; the bottom row regresses the within-meeting standard deviation of sentiment on the cross-maturity mean of $|\Delta\text{OIS}|$.

\begin{figure}[htbp]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{images/results/model/fig_delta_sentiment.pdf}
    \caption[Communication surprise and market volatility]{Top row: change in sentiment versus the change in \gls{ois}-$1$Y during the press-conference window. Bottom row: within-meeting standard deviation of sentiment versus the cross-maturity mean of $|\Delta\text{OIS}|$. Points are colour-coded by era. Regression lines shown for visual reference.}
    \label{fig:delta_sentiment}
\end{figure}

The results echo Section~\ref{sec:divergence}: the directional correlations between communication surprise and the directional OIS move are statistically indistinguishable from zero (top row, all $|r| < 0.05$), but the standard deviation of sentiment is mildly negatively correlated with the absolute size of the OIS move ($r = -0.137$ for the CentralBankRoBERTa overall standard deviation, $p < 0.05$, bottom-right panel). The sign mirrors the communicative-cancellation finding: more internally variable communication is associated with smaller market reactions. This is consistent with a market that interprets internal variability as an absence of decisive policy news and therefore declines to revise its rate expectations.

\paragraph{Summary of Chapter Findings.}
Across the four arcs of analysis, four substantive findings have emerged. First, the two sentiment classifiers produce coherent, mutually validating signals that align with the major historical turning points of euro-area monetary policy. Second, the prepared \gls{is} and the spontaneous \gls{qa} are structurally different communicative acts, and the journalist--\gls{ecb} gap is a stable institutional feature that, if not separated by the pipeline, would have contaminated the policy signal by approximately $0.3$ on the unit scale. Third, sentiment leads the \gls{ecb}'s rate decisions by between two and ten meetings, with the lead being long and gradual in the pre-\gls{zlb} era and approximately symmetric in the post-\gls{zlb} hiking cycle. Fourth, sentiment features carry incremental predictive content beyond the rate history itself: AUC rises from $0.68$ for the rate-history baseline to $0.72$--$0.74$ once sentiment and its non-linear interactions are added. The implications of these findings for policy makers, for the design of future sentiment pipelines, and for the limitations that remain are discussed in Chapter~\ref{ch:discussion}.
