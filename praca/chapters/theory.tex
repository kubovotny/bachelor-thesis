\chapter{Theoretical Background}\label{chap:theory}
This chapter establishes the~theoretical foundation for the~research in this thesis by connecting the~macroeconomic concepts of \gls{mp} with advanced
language technology. The~first section looks at the~evolution of central bank communication. It looks at the~mechanics of the \gls{mps}, the~distinct
roles of prepared speeches versus Q\&A sessions, and how they communicate between meetings. The~second section introduces \gls{nlp} and how it is used
in the~financial domain, providing the~methodological background for extracting useful information from unstructured central bank texts. It covers the
shift from traditional dictionary-based sentiment analysis to transformer models, such as BERT and its domain-specific adaptations. The~last sections
explain the~theoretical basics of time series analysis and portfolio optimization, which are essential for the~practical application of the~extracted
sentiment signals in financial forecasting and the~development of investment strategies.

\section{Central Bank Communication}
Over the~past three decades, central banking has undergone profound and irreversible changes in the~structure of its disclosures. In the~past, central
banks shrouded their operations in secrecy. Before the~1990s, there was no public explanation of why monetary policy decisions were made. Policymakers
believed that their decisions should surprise the~market because they believed that this was essential for the effectiveness of policy interventions.
This period of institutional unpredictability, famously defined by the~“Secrets of the~Temple” mentality during the~early Alan Greenspan's tenure
at the~\gls{fed}—the~central bank of the~United States, was characterized by a~marked lack of formal public guidance \cite[p. 3]{NBERw11898}. Market
participants repeatedly had to guess the~target interest rate and the~bank's policy setting. They relied on the~size, timing, and nature of
the~operations conducted by the~\gls{fed} trading departments in the~open market.

Since then, however, there has been a~fundamental paradigm shift that repositioned communication from a~peripheral public relations function to
a~core, independent instrument of \gls{mp}. Public institutions have an inherent duty to share information, and central banks have increasingly
recognized that transparency and accountability are fundamental public service obligations \cite[p. 15--18]{blinder2008central}. Beyond mere
institutional accountability itself, the~evolution of \gls{mp} frameworks has demonstrated that transparent and credible central banks are the~solid
basis for long-term price stability \cite{daly2025dynamic}. Particularly, the~widespread adoption of inflation targeting across advanced and emerging
economies. Empirically, clear communication has been shown to enhance central bank independence, strengthen institutional credibility, and actively
anchor inflation expectations in complex macroeconomic environments \cite[p. 7]{filardo2008transparency}.

\subsection{Monetary Policy Statement}\label{subsection:mps}
At the~operational core of the~\gls{ecb}'s communication strategy is the~\gls{mps}, which is delivered during the~press conference that directly
follows the~\gls{mp} meetings of the~\gls{gc}. The~council consists of the~six members of the~Executive Board (including the~President and
Vice-President) and the~governors of the~euro area national central banks, who meet to deliberate and set monetary policy parameters for the~entire
currency bloc. The~\gls{ecb} currently holds eight dedicated \gls{mp} meetings per year, operating on an approximate six-week cycle
\cite{ITC_ECB_Schedule}.

\subsubsection{Schedule of the Governing Council Meeting Day}
The~organization of the~announcement day is highly standardized, reflecting the~institution's strict commitment to predictability and to mitigating
of market volatility. Starting from July 21, 2022, the~\gls{ecb} modified its historical publication timeline to optimize the~way financial markets
process complex policy signals \cite{ECB2022_NewTimes}. The~initial policy decision—usually a~short press release detailing the~exact interest rate
parameters, deposit facility rates, and main structural changes to asset purchase programs—is published at 14:15 CET. Shortly afterward, the~press
conference begins at 14:45 CET, during which the~\gls{ecb} President and Vice-President present the~detailed \gls{mps} and interact directly with the
financial press. In parallel with the~start of the~Q\&A segment, the~full written text of the~\gls{mps} is published on the~\gls{ecb}'s digital
platforms at 15:00 CET. At 17:00, European stock markets close independently of the~\gls{ecb}, ending the~day's reactions to the~\gls{ecb} actions.

\subsubsection{Structure of the~Press Conference}
The~press conference itself is divided into two distinct communication phases: the~reading of the~formally prepared introductory statement and
the~subsequent unscripted Question-and-Answer (Q\&A) session. This structural division is not merely procedural; it has profound empirical
implications for financial markets, as the~two sections serve completely different epistemological functions and generate distinct asset price
dynamics.

The~introductory statement is a~highly structured, carefully drafted document that represents the~\gls{gc}'s absolute consensus view.
\cite{ECB_WP2742}. It offers a~comprehensive, linear overview of the~integrated analytical framework that progressively details the~economic
assessment, inflation dynamics, and the~evaluation of monetary and financial conditions. The~language used in this document is deliberately precise,
relying on specific syntactical formulations to signal the~future policy path—a practice commonly known as forward-looking policy setting.

In sharp contrast, the~Q\&A session introduces a controlled spontaneity. Advanced textual analysis and structural topic modeling of the~historical
transcripts of \gls{ecb} press conferences demonstrate that the~thematic content of the~statement differs from the discourse during the~Q\&A session.
The~topics that dominate the~statement are persistent, structural, and they arestrongly focused on standardized macroeconomic aggregates. Conversely,
the~Q\&A often introduces new topics that were not addressed in the~prepared text. Empirical research reveals that the~first six most prevalent topics
within a~vast sample of press conference transcripts are predominantly driven by the~Q\&A segments. This suggests that the~true added value of
the~press conference lies largely in the~Q\&A phase, which provides the~public and market participants with detailed explanations regarding
the~\gls{ecb}'s decisions in real time \cite{ECB_WP2742}.

\subsection{Other Communication Channels}
While the~\gls{mps} and the~accompanying press conference represent the~focal point of the~\gls{ecb}'s communication apparatus, they operate within
a~much broader, continuously active ecosystem of publications, reports, and public engagements. To continuously steer the~yield curve, anchor
long-term expectations, and fulfill its mandate of transparency, the~\gls{ecb} leverages several supplementary communication channels in the~weeks
between formal \gls{gc} meetings. This ``inter-meeting communication'' is a~powerful tool for addressing structural complexities and the~mechanics of
financial transmission. These details can not be mentioned in a~single monthly press conference \cite{Istrefi2021ECB}.

\subsubsection{The~Economic Bulletin}
The~Economic Bulletin is a~comprehensive, data-rich analytical publication that serves to fully explain the~reasons behind the~\gls{ecb}'s Governing
Council's monetary policy decisions. Published eight times a~year—generally two weeks after the~corresponding \gls{mp} meeting—the~Bulletin
contextualizes the~Governing Council's decisions against the~detailed backdrop of prevailing economic, financial, and monetary conditions
\cite{press_to_speeches}.

\subsubsection{The~Monetary Policy Accounts}
In a~major step toward heightened transparency, the~ECB began publishing regular accounts of the~Governing Council's \gls{mp} meetings in early 2015,
aligning its institutional practices with those of the~US \gls{fed} and the~Bank of England. These accounts are typically released four weeks after
the~corresponding meeting \cite{MP_accounts}.

The~documents offer a~detailed, synthesized overview of the~Governing Council's internal deliberations. They outline the~delicate balance of
arguments, the~range of risk assessments considered by various members, and the~different perspectives on the~economic outlook. By documenting
the~full range of analytical debates, the~accounts help market participants understand the~central bank's internal response function and the~specific
macroeconomic thresholds that might trigger future policy interventions \cite[p. 26--27]{CorbisieroLawton2021}.

\section{Natural Language Processing in Finance}
The~exponential growth of the~volume of digital information has fundamentally transformed the~methodology of financial and macroeconomic research.
Historically, empirical finance has relied almost exclusively on structured, quantitative data. However, language has always shaped financial markets,
and unstructured textual data—ranging from earnings call transcripts to central bank \gls{mps}—contains critical information about the~future.
\gls{nlp} serves as a~technological bridge, enabling researchers and practitioners to systematically extract structured, machine-readable signals from
these vast corpora of unstructured text \cite{IBM_IE_2025}. In the~context of \gls{mp}, \gls{nlp} algorithms are used to quantify the~qualitative
narratives of central bankers, capturing nuances in market psychology and institutional sentiment.

\subsection{Concept of Sentiment Analysis in Finance}
Sentiment analysis is the~computational extraction of subjective information and emotional states from text, primarily focused on classifying
a document's underlying polarity \cite{xing2020financial}. In general, this polarity is traditionally categorized into positive, neutral, or
negative dimensions. Early \gls{nlp} applications relied heavily on dictionary-based approaches, which classify texts by simply counting the~frequency
of predefined positive or negative terms. However, standard baseline dictionaries routinely misclassify common financial terminology; for example,
the~word ``liability'' is negative in a~general context, but serves as a~neutral, descriptive accounting term in financial filings. This difference
has necessitated the~development of domain-specific lexicons, such as the~Loughran-McDonald Master Dictionary \cite{LoughranMcDonald2011}, to accurately
measure sentiment in corporate contexts.

\subsection{Transformer Models}
While domain-specific dictionaries provided basic insights, they inherently struggle to capture complex syntax, context, and linguistic nuances.
Algorithms that used Master Dictionary-based models, which process words in isolation (called Bag-of-words models), did not capture contextual
relationships. So the~introduction of deep learning, and more specifically, transformer-based neural network architectures, has improved financial
\gls{nlp}. At the~forefront of this shift was BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from Transformers). A~language model that processes words in
relation to all other words in a~sentence.

Despite its power, general-purpose BERT was trained on general-domain texts (such as Wikipedia) and often struggled with the~highly specialized
lexicon of finance \cite[p. 6]{BIS_WP1215_2024}. This limitation led to the~development of FinBERT \cite{araci2019finbert}, a~model fine-tuned
extensively on financial texts, which significantly outperformed general-purpose BERT in accurately classifying the~sentiment of professional
financial discourse.

However, the field of central banking verbal expression is even more specialized. FinBERT was trained mostly on financial news, specifically on
a~large subset of the~Reuters TRC2 dataset. These texts are less formal and less institutionally independent than central bank texts. That was
one of the reasons to introduce models such as CentralBankRoBERTa and other Central Bank Language Models (CB-LMs). These models were trained
explicitly on specialized texts, including central bank speeches, \gls{mps}, and macroeconomic research papers \cite[p. 4]{BIS_WP1215_2024}. Empirical
benchmarks show that CB-LMs significantly outperform both standard and financial models in understanding central bank idioms. In central banking,
instead of a~positive vs. negative stance, it is replaced by hawkish vs. dovish stances. Which describes policymaker's stances on monetary policy.
Where hawkish indicates expectations to be more on the tightening side, and dovish on the loosening side. Therefore, hawkish is a~supplement for
positive and dovish for negative. CB-LMs classified the~hawkish, dovish, or neutral stance of official policy communications more accurately than
FinBERT. By using these highly calibrated transformer models, researchers can extract more precise, theory-consistent signals from institutions like
the~\gls{ecb} and the~\gls{fed}. This improved the~predictability of market reactions and policy transmission.
