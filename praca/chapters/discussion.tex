\chapter{Discussion}\label{ch:discussion}

The empirical findings of Chapter~\ref{ch:results} establish that the textual signal carried by the \gls{ecb}'s press conferences is internally coherent, structurally heterogeneous between its prepared and spontaneous components, leading rather than coincident with the policy rate, and weakly but systematically informative about the direction of the next rate decision. This chapter discusses what these findings imply for policy makers and market participants (Section~\ref{sec:implications}), what they reveal about the methodological choices that shaped them (Section~\ref{sec:methodological_reflections}), the limitations that bound their interpretation (Section~\ref{sec:limitations}), and the most natural extensions of the framework (Section~\ref{sec:future_work}).

\section{Implications for Policy Makers and Markets}
\label{sec:implications}

The headline empirical regularity of the thesis is that affective tone leads the policy rate. The event study of Section~\ref{sec:event_study} shows a pre-\gls{zlb} pattern in which a hawkish profile is established up to six meetings before an actual hike, and a post-\gls{zlb} pattern in which both hikes and cuts are symmetrically telegraphed across the same window. The lag-correlation analysis of Section~\ref{sec:results_correlations} corroborates this finding in a non-event-study setting and identifies a peak correlation between the FinBERT overall mean and the change in the \gls{mro} rate at a lag of approximately five meetings. From the perspective of a market participant, this means that the textual record of \gls{ecb} communications is a leading indicator of the price of money, not a contemporaneous one. From the perspective of a policy maker, it means that the communication strategy of the post-pandemic \gls{ecb}---a strategy of long, symmetric telegraphing of both directions of policy change---is reflected in the price formation of euro-area rate expectations to a measurable degree.

A second implication concerns the structural division of the press conference. Section~\ref{sec:results_arc} demonstrated that the prepared \gls{is} has a coherent three-act narrative arc in which the most market-relevant policy content is concentrated at the end, while the \gls{qa} has no narrative arc at all and is best characterised as a flat reactive dialogue. The implication for policy makers is that the communication content of the introductory statement is, in effect, a structured argument with a known position for the conclusion: a market that has learned this structure should already be paying disproportionate attention to the closing paragraphs of the \gls{is}. The implication for market participants is the symmetric one: the affective tone of the \gls{qa}, while not without informational value, should be read as a divergence signal relative to the prepared text rather than as a signal in its own right. This is consistent with the communicative-cancellation finding of Section~\ref{sec:divergence}, in which the largest \gls{is}--\gls{qa} divergences were associated with the \emph{smallest} OIS reactions: the market interprets internal disagreement as a sign that no clean policy update is on offer and consequently declines to update.

A third implication concerns the structural pessimism of the journalist corps. Section~\ref{sec:results_gap} documented that journalist questions are systematically more dovish than the \gls{ecb}'s answers by an average of $0.29$ on the unit scale, and that this gap is stable across structurally different monetary regimes. The institutional reading is clear: the press corps is professionally rewarded for surfacing downside risks, and the central bank is constitutionally bound to project measured stability; the gap is a structural feature of the discourse rather than a measurement error. The practical implication for any future sentiment-based research on central bank communication is that the question--answer separation is not an optional refinement but a methodological prerequisite: without it, the affective tone of the press corps contaminates the policy signal by an order of magnitude that is large relative to the policy-relevant variation in the signal.

\section{Methodological Reflections}
\label{sec:methodological_reflections}

The empirical results of Chapter~\ref{ch:results} cannot be separated from the methodological choices that produced them. Three choices deserve explicit reflection.

\paragraph{Sentiment versus stance.}
The framework adopted in this thesis measures the \emph{affective tone} of \gls{ecb} discourse rather than its \emph{policy stance}. The distinction matters. Sentiment classifiers assign a positive/negative polarity to a passage on the basis of its emotional valence; stance classifiers assign a hawkish/dovish/neutral label on the basis of its policy implication. The two need not coincide. A sentence such as \textit{``inflation is far too high and we are determined to bring it back to target''} carries a strongly negative emotional valence (alarm about inflation) but implies a hawkish policy stance (a commitment to tighter policy). The \gls{zlb}-era flatness of the sentiment series (Section~\ref{sec:results_comovement}) is a direct manifestation of this mismatch. The language of unconventional easing---\textit{``asset purchase programme''}, \textit{``reinvestment''}, \textit{``favourable financing conditions''}---is structurally different from the language of conventional rate cuts, and a classifier whose training distribution is dominated by the latter will not register the former as dovish. State-of-the-art stance classifiers fine-tuned specifically on the \gls{fed} and the \gls{ecb} \cite{shah2023trillion, shah2025wcb} would in principle close this gap, but they are released under gated access on the Hugging Face Hub and could not be obtained within the timeframe of this study. The signal extracted here should therefore be read as a measure of affective tone, and the \gls{zlb} flatness as a property of the training distribution of the classifiers rather than of the \gls{ecb}'s communications.

\paragraph{Granularity and the chunk-size trade-off.}
Section~\ref{sec:segmentation} reported that the cross-model correlation between FinBERT and CentralBankRoBERTa is highest at single-sentence granularity and decreases monotonically as the chunk size grows, while the within-meeting standard deviation flattens beyond $200$ words. The trade-off is genuine. Short chunks are evaluated more consistently by the two encoders because their context window is shorter and the encoders are forced to rely on lexical cues that they both share, but the same shortness fragments coherent economic arguments into many decontextualised fragments and erodes the very contextual reasoning that motivated the use of transformer encoders in the first place. The $200$-word setting adopted as the headline configuration trades stability for context. The single-sentence configuration used in the predictive analysis of Section~\ref{sec:results_prediction} trades context for predictive power on a small sample. Neither configuration is uniformly superior, and the choice is best understood as a function of the question rather than as a methodological invariant.

\paragraph{Topic taxonomy.}
Section~\ref{sec:topic_classification} reported that the initial ten-topic taxonomy resulted in decision paralysis for the zero-shot classifier and was therefore simplified to four core themes with descriptive hypothesis strings. This simplification is a pragmatic compromise rather than a principled choice. A four-topic taxonomy is too coarse to distinguish, for example, between forward guidance and inflation analysis within the broader Monetary Policy category, and the resulting topic-conditioned sentiment series are correspondingly less informative than they could be at a finer granularity. The robustness check with a second NLI backbone (DeBERTa-v3 trained on a broader collection of NLI corpora) attenuates this concern by demonstrating that the classifier output is not an artefact of the specific encoder, but it does not resolve the underlying limitation that the four-topic taxonomy reflects what zero-shot classification can do reliably rather than what the empirical question would ideally require. A custom-trained topic model on a domain-specific labelled corpus would resolve this limitation and is identified as a priority extension in Section~\ref{sec:future_work}.

\paragraph{Linear baselines for a small-sample problem.}
The predictive analysis of Section~\ref{sec:results_prediction} relies on a multinomial logistic regression as the primary specification and a random forest as a non-linear robustness check. The choice of a linear baseline reflects the small sample of changed meetings ($n \approx 56$ over $25$ years) rather than a methodological preference. With $56$ effective observations and the four-feature set used in models M2 and M3, the effective ratio of observations to parameters is at the edge of the range in which logistic regression remains identifiable, and richer architectures (gradient-boosted trees, neural networks) would face an acute over-fitting risk. The random forest M4 was included precisely because it is the simplest non-linear specification with a built-in mechanism for handling small samples (bootstrap aggregation, restricted depth) and provides the most defensible test of whether the linear specification is leaving signal on the table. The marginal AUC gain of M4 over M3 is positive but modest, consistent with the interpretation that the predictive content of the sentiment features is largely linear at the sample size of this study.

\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

Four limitations bound the interpretation of the results above.

\paragraph{Sample size.}
The sample consists of $272$ press conferences over the $1999$--$2024$ window. Of these, only approximately $56$ involve a change in the \gls{mro} rate, the remaining $216$ being either holds or covered by the \gls{zlb} flatness. The predictive analysis of Section~\ref{sec:results_prediction} is therefore conditioned on a small number of decision events, which limits both the statistical power available for the predictive task and the granularity at which non-linear interactions between rate history and sentiment can be reliably detected. The $5$-fold time-series cross-validation procedure mitigates this concern relative to a single train--test split but does not eliminate it: each fold contains a relatively small number of changed meetings, and the resulting AUC estimates carry a non-trivial across-fold standard deviation.

\paragraph{Single central bank.}
The analysis is restricted to the \gls{ecb}. The institutional structure of the \gls{ecb}'s communications---the standardised \gls{is}, the structured \gls{qa}, the schedule of eight \gls{mp} meetings per year, and the multi-channel inter-meeting communication apparatus---differs in non-trivial ways from those of the Federal Reserve, the Bank of England, or other major central banks. The findings of this thesis should therefore be read as statements about the \gls{ecb}, not about central bank communication in general. The framework, however, is portable: the scraping pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:scraping} could be adapted to other central banks without methodological changes, and a cross-bank replication is identified as a natural extension in Section~\ref{sec:future_work}.

\paragraph{Classifier domain mismatch in the ZLB era.}
The pre-trained sentiment classifiers used in this thesis were fine-tuned on corpora in which the language of unconventional monetary policy is under-represented. Their flatness during the \gls{zlb} window (Section~\ref{sec:results_comovement}) is a documented limitation rather than a finding about the \gls{ecb}'s communicative behaviour. The mismatch could in principle be resolved by training a sentiment classifier on \gls{ecb} discourse from the \gls{zlb} era specifically, but the absence of a domain-specific labelled sentiment dataset for this period rules out such an extension within the present timeframe. The implication for the empirical chapters is that the \gls{zlb} window should be interpreted with extra caution: the underlying policy intent during that period was strongly accommodative, but the classifier output does not reflect this intent.

\paragraph{Affective scope.}
Section~\ref{sec:methodological_reflections} already noted that the framework captures sentiment rather than stance. A second affective limitation is that the binary positive/negative polarity discards the third \textit{neutral} class. The aggregation operator of Equation~\ref{eq:sentiment} maps neutrally classified chunks to zero, attenuating the aggregate signal toward zero whenever the discourse is procedural, factual, or descriptive rather than evaluative. The size of this attenuation is bounded above by the proportion of chunks classified as neutral, which is non-trivial for routine press conferences but small for crisis-era press conferences. The effect is therefore strongest in exactly those periods in which the signal is least diagnostic.

\section{Future Work and Groundwork for Publication}
\label{sec:future_work}

The framework of this thesis is designed to be extended. Four extensions are particularly natural.

% In §4.4, replace the stance paragraph with:
\paragraph{Stance classification.}
The single most informative extension would replace the affective-sentiment layer with a dedicated stance classifier. The gated classifiers of Shah et al.\ \cite{shah2023trillion, shah2025wcb} represent the current state of the art, but their restricted access on the Hugging Face Hub could not be obtained within this study's timeframe, and no collaboration with the authors was pursued. Two independent paths remain open. First, a custom stance classifier can be trained from scratch on a manually annotated subset of the existing ECB chunk corpus using the annotation protocol of  \cite{shah2023trillion}; a corpus of approximately $2{,}000$ labelled chunks would be sufficient for fine-tuning a RoBERTa-base encoder and would require only a modest annotation effort. Second, a proprietary Large Language Model (LLM) (e.g.\ GPT-4o or Claude) can serve as a zero-shot stance annotator with a carefully designed prompt, providing a high-quality silver-standard label set for distillation into a smaller, deployable encoder. This second path is the most modern approach currently available, though it introduces a critical caveat: LLM outputs are \emph{non-deterministic} unless temperature is fixed at zero, meaning the same chunk can receive different labels across runs; any pipeline that uses an LLM as a labeller must therefore fix temperature and log the exact model version to ensure reproducibility.

\paragraph{Fine-tuning the topic and stance classifiers.}
The zero-shot topic classifier is limited by the expressiveness of its four hypothesis strings and the general-purpose NLI pre-training of its backbone. A labelled corpus of ECB chunks would unlock two improvements. First, the topic model can be fine-tuned directly: starting from \texttt{facebook/bart-large-mnli} or \texttt{DeBERTa-v3} and continuing training on a domain-specific NLI dataset (premise = ECB chunk; hypothesis = topic string; label = entail/neutral/contradict) would substantially narrow the classifier's uncertainty on ambiguous passages. Second, a fine-tuned stance classifier---trained on the same corpus with hawkish/dovish/neutral labels---would subsume both tasks, providing a single model that jointly assigns policy intent and thematic focus. The modular database architecture of Section~\ref{sec:db} supports both extensions without schema changes: new  \texttt{topic\_model} rows and a new \texttt{version} on \texttt{topic\_labels}  accommodate additional label sets, and the \texttt{model\_id} foreign key on \texttt{topics} allows results from old and new classifiers to coexist in the same store for direct comparison.

\paragraph{Intraday market windows.}
The market panel used in this thesis aggregates the OIS reaction at the daily level. The EA-MPD dataset \cite{altavilla2019measuring} also provides intraday windows around the press release ($14$:$15$ CET) and around the press conference ($14$:$45$--$15$:$15$ CET). Separating these windows would allow the predictive analysis to test whether the affective tone of the \gls{is} (read during the $15$:$00$ window) is incorporated into rates faster than the affective tone of the \gls{qa} (which evolves through the $15$:$00$--$15$:$45$ window). The hypothesis that the \gls{qa} signal is the slower-incorporating one is consistent with the divergence finding of Section~\ref{sec:divergence} and is testable with the existing data.

\paragraph{Custom topic model.}
The four-topic taxonomy used in this thesis was selected for compatibility with zero-shot classification rather than as the ideal economic taxonomy. A custom topic model trained on a domain-specific labelled corpus---for example, by manual annotation of a stratified sample of chunks against a richer set of monetary-policy categories (forward guidance, inflation analysis, financial-conditions assessment, fiscal coordination, etc.)---would yield a more granular thematic decomposition and reopen the topic-conditional analyses that were deferred in this thesis. The labelled corpus required for such an extension is modest in size (a few thousand chunks) and is well within the scope of a follow-up study.

\paragraph{Cross-bank replication and NBS publication groundwork.}
The pipeline is portable across central banks. A natural follow-up study would replicate the analysis on the press conferences of the Federal Reserve and the Bank of England under identical chunking, classification, and aggregation rules, allowing a structured comparison of the lead--lag profile, the journalist--bank gap, and the predictive content of sentiment across the three institutions. From the perspective of the planned co-authored publication with the Národná banka Slovenska, this cross-bank replication is the most direct policy-relevant extension: the cross-bank profile of communication asymmetry would directly inform the conditions under which a small open economy facing the euro area should treat \gls{ecb} sentiment as a leading indicator versus a contemporaneous signal. The codebase developed for this thesis (the scraping pipeline of Section~\ref{sec:scraping}, the database schema of Section~\ref{sec:db}, the chunk-to-meeting aggregation operator of Section~\ref{sec:aggregation}, and the predictive framework of Section~\ref{sec:results_prediction}) is structured as a configurable framework precisely to support this extension.

\paragraph{Concluding remark.}
The four findings of Chapter~\ref{ch:results}---internal coherence of the two sentiment series, structural heterogeneity between \gls{is} and \gls{qa}, lead--lag asymmetry of sentiment around rate decisions, and incremental predictive value of sentiment beyond the rate history---together establish that the textual record of \gls{ecb} press conferences carries economically meaningful information beyond what is contained in the policy rate itself. The methodological framework that produced these findings is portable and extensible, and the limitations identified above describe extensions of scope rather than barriers to the central conclusion.
